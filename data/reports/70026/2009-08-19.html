Community Page

The Education Review Office [ERO] evaluation of Belfast Playcentre follows.

Belfast Playcentre is one of 51 playcentres administered by the Canterbury Playcentre Association. The playcentre operates within the constitution of the association. Each centre operates as a parent cooperative that is responsible for the day-to-day operation of the centre.

At the time of the 2008 ERO review, a small group of new parents was exploring the playcentre philosophy and how they could fulfil the roles and responsibilities necessary to run the centre. The report identified the effort parents were putting in to improving the quality of the programme and centre operations. It also identified a number of areas for improvement.

This report outlines the progress the centre has made in addressing these areas. They include adult and child interactions, the learning environment, developing a bicultural perspective, assessment planning and evaluation, and a range of areas requiring improvement to health and safety procedures and practices.

Parents continue to support each other well. They have a good understanding of their roles and responsibilities and work cooperatively to ensure that the centre runs smoothly. Parents establish warm and supportive relationships with each other‚Äôs children and respond to their needs and play activities. Parents are aware of the need to extend their interactions with children to focus more on extending learning, oral language and creativity.

The learning environment has been significantly improved. The interior has been reorganised and redecorated to create improved learning areas and comfort for children. Play equipment and toys are being replaced and improved. The outside environment is now fenced and parents have plans to further landscape parts of the outdoor environment.

The environment better reflects a bicultural perspective. Te reo M√§ori is used to greet children, provide comments, give instructions, and in singing waiata. Parents‚Äô confidence and competence with te reo M√§ori could be developed with further training and support.

Parents are improving their understanding and use of learning stories to better support children‚Äôs learning. Parents are aware of the importance of evaluating learning and identifying next learning steps for each child. Their next step is to develop a process for gathering and using information from a variety of sources to help them evaluate the progress children are making in their learning. This includes using learning stories, the daily reflections of parents and the outcomes of self review. This information could also help the supervisor to further develop a process for programme planning. Shared understandings about the programme should improve the quality and consistency for children‚Äôs learning.

Parents have addressed the non-compliance areas identified in the 2008 ERO review.

Future Action

ERO is confident that the service is being managed in the interests of the children. Therefore ERO will review the service again as part of the regular review cycle.

When ERO has reviewed an early childhood centre we encourage management to inform their community of any follow-up action they plan to do. You should talk to the management or licensee if you have any questions about this evaluation, the full ERO report or their future intentions.

If you would like a copy of the full report, please contact the centre or see the ERO website,¬†http://www.ero.govt.nz.

1. Background

Introduction

A Supplementary Review is undertaken at the discretion of an Area Manager in the Education Review Office (ERO).

A supplementary review evaluates the extent and effectiveness of actions a centre has taken towards addressing issues specified in a previous education review and/or any additional areas identified since that review.

This supplementary review report was prepared in accordance with standard procedures approved by the Chief Review Officer.

Terms of Reference

This supplementary review is based on an evaluation of the performance of the Belfast Playcentre governing body and management in relation to areas identified in the ERO report or issues identified since that review. The terms of reference for this review are to investigate:

  • adult and child interactions;
  • the learning environment;
  • bicultural perspective;
  • assessment, planning and evaluation;
  • health and safety; and
  • any other issues relevant to the governance, management and operation of the centre arising during the course of the review.

3. Findings

Adult and Child Interactions

Background

The 2008 ERO report identified the need for adults to develop a clear and shared understanding of their role in supporting children‚Äôs play during the session. Many parents were not effectively engaging with children in ways that would support and further develop their learning.

Areas of progress

  • Parents supporting each other. The centre parents are very supportive of each other and their children. Roles and responsibilities of parents have been clarified. Communication between them is clear, supportive and caring. Parents make sure that each person contributes to caring for and maintaining the centre. Centre responsibilities are allocated and there is a good level of accountability. Parents support each other through the sharing of information about their parenting role.
  • Adults responding to children. Adults notice children‚Äôs play and respond with support and encouragement. The high ratio of parent-to-child makes it possible for frequent interactions between adults and children. Parents talk to children about their activities and often show them ways of using equipment and materials. They follow children‚Äôs interests and encourage cooperative play.

Areas for further improvement

  • Extending interactions with children to foster learning. Parents do not always interact with children in ways that foster further thinking and learning. Children engage well in activities. However, some parent-child interactions were short and did not help children to extend their thinking and learning. The next step for parents is to improve their discussion and questioning skills so that children are encouraged to explore, experiment and create more, and engage in imaginative play when appropriate.
  • Promoting children‚Äôs oral language. Parents are providing good role models for children‚Äôs oral language. However, they do not always take time to give children the opportunity to talk, discuss and ask questions. Further training through Playcentre training courses would give parents help in developing these skills and to evaluate the beneficial effect they could have on children‚Äôs language development.

The Learning Environment

Background

The poor quality of resources was an area for improvement in the 2008 ERO review. Puzzles and toys were in poor repair. There was a limited range of resources for babies. Children did not always have easy access to equipment.

Area of progress

  • The learning environment. Children and their parents learn and play in a spacious well-resourced physical environment. Parents have made good progress in reviewing and refurbishing the inside environment to better meet children‚Äôs learning needs. They have repainted the inside area in a colour to enhance wall displays. New notice boards now display information for parents, the centre philosophy, and explanations of Te WhƒÅriki, the New¬†Zealand Early Childhood Curriculum. Examples of assessments model good practice for parents. The play areas are clearly set out and better organised to allow easy access for children. Children now have new lockers for their lunch boxes and profile books. Resources and equipment are in the process of being upgraded. The outside area continues to be a well-maintained and well resourced area. A gate and new fencing have been built. Children enjoy opportunities to climb, ride bikes, play in the sandpit and use their carpentry skills. Parents are considering further landscaping in one outside area.

Bicultural Perspective

Background

The centre did not have a statement of practice, as required by their association, that demonstrates how bicultural practices should be incorporated into the programme. The centre environment did not reflect the bicultural nature of New¬†Zealand. Parents did not feel confident in using te reo M√§ori.

Area of progress

  • Initiatives. The centre is making progress in implementing the association‚Äôs expectations for reflecting a bicultural perspective. Te reo M√§ori is frequently used for greetings, songs and instructions. Children‚Äôs profile books also use te reo M√§ori headings.

Area for further improvement

  • Developing parent confidence. Parents need further support to improve their confidence in te reo and tikanga M√§ori. The coordinator uses te reo M√§ori and encourages parents to do so. Further initiatives and support should help parents feel more competent and confident using the language.

Assessment, Planning and Evaluation

Background

At the time of the 2008 ERO review, planning, assessment and evaluation practices were not recognising and supporting the needs of individual children. Profile books were of poor quality. Programme planning was not clearly linked to children‚Äôs interests and learning needs. Programmes were not being evaluated.

Area of progress

  • Learning stories. Parents have improved the quality of the learning stories they write to support children‚Äôs ongoing learning. They are now focussing more on the learning that is happening during activities. Some parents can identify next steps for learning. As a result, children‚Äôs individual needs can be responded to and future learning opportunities planned. While there has been good progress in improving learning stories, there is need for further development. The training opportunities available to parents are an important process in helping them to understand and effectively use learning stories to guide and support their children‚Äôs learning.

Areas for further improvement

  • Evaluation of learning. The parents have yet to develop a shared process for recognising and evaluating children‚Äôs learning and identifying next learning steps. They record some evidence in individual learning stories. Some parents complete an evaluation form at the end of a session. It is not clear how findings from self review are used to evaluate the programme. The next step is for parents to identify the evidence they need to evaluate the effectiveness of the programme in improving learning, and where they will find that evidence. This can then be used as the basis for further programme improvements to meet the needs of children.

  • Programme planning process. Parents do not have an agreed process for programme planning. There are insufficient written guidelines that will help parents effectively identify and share information that will be used to plan each day‚Äôs programme. It is difficult for parents to gain a shared understanding at the beginning of a session of what will happen and how they can best support all children. Some parents are aware of the need for all parents to have a shared and consistent approach and plan to share ideas and improve this process.

Health and Safety

The areas of non-compliance related to health and safety requirements identified in the 2008 ERO review have been addressed.

4. Recommendations

ERO recommends that:

  1. the supervisor and parents extend their interactions with children to improve children‚Äôs thinking, learning and creativity;
  2. the supervisor and parents develop shared understanding of processes for evaluating children‚Äôs learning and planning programmes to meet children‚Äôs learning needs; and
  3. parents are supported to improve their understanding and use of te reo and tikanga M√§ori.

5. Future Action

ERO is confident that the service is being managed in the interests of the children. Therefore ERO will review the service again as part of the regular review cycle.
