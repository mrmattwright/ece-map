ERO's findings that support this overall judgement are summarised below.

Background

Tamariki Takitumu Punanga Reo in Mangere is licensed for 30 children, including up to five under two years of age. Most of the children are of Cook Islands MƒÅori descent but few have the opportunity to speak te reo o Rarotonga in their homes. Following consultation with their parents, the centre has moved from total immersion to a multi-lingual curriculum.

Teacher-to-child ratios are high. Children under two have their own well-resourced learning spaces and spend a lot of time in the larger learning spaces of their tuƒÅkana.

The centre's philosophy has changed very little since opening in 2004 and provides a solid foundation for the positive outcomes for children that guide teaching practice.

Since the 2014 ERO report there have been many personnel changes, with the appointment of a new centre manager, new teachers and several new trustees including a new chairperson. The elected trust board meets monthly to oversee the governance of the centre and receive management reports from the centre manager and teaching staff. Trustees and centre leaders have responded well to areas for improvement identified in the 2014 ERO report. These related to staff appraisal, self review, curriculum management, financial management and police vetting processes. Some progress has been made in these areas.

The Review Findings

Tamariki, metua and ƒÅnau are warmly welcomed each day into a culturally rich Rarotongan setting. Children are keen to renew friendships as they engage in activities of choice while metua, ƒÅnau and teachers share information about children's learning.

Teachers engage with children, using respectful, warm, responsive interactions in te reo o Rarotonga. The environment has a home-like and calm atmosphere, providing a sense of security and wellbeing for children. Outdoor challenges provide good opportunities for children to develop physically. Children are settled, relaxed and engaged in the programme as they follow the things they are curious about to explore and delve into play activities.

Babies and toddlers are provided for in a separate area where their needs are met by the programme. Adults warmly affirm children as they independently explore the resources available to them. Babies and toddlers benefit from opportunities to mix with older children. Teachers need to align the planning and assessment practices in the babies' and toddlers' area with what happens for the older children.

Children know the routines well. They freely choose to play indoors or outdoors, initiate their own play and learning, often in friendly groups. There are opportunities for them to form relationships with other children while being cared for by attentive teachers, who also support them to explore aspects of literacy and numeracy. Teachers should now consider how they could extend children‚Äôs thinking and bring more complexity to their play through skilful questioning.

The programme is currently based around children‚Äôs group interests, special days on the national calendar and important cultural celebrations. Children's portfolios record their participation in these programmes, celebrating each child as a member of the wider centre whƒÅnau.

Leaders recognise that key next steps are to focus more on extending the interests of individual children and to involve parents in assessment, planning and evaluation processes. Targeted professional development will help teachers to identify their role in children's learning and make links to Te WhƒÅriki, the early childhood curriculum.

The centre manager promotes improvement. Leaders continue to work on refining appraisal processes and on ensuring that effective teaching supports children's learning across the centre. More in-depth professional development for leaders will support them to embed a more robust performance management process.

Trustees bring a broad range of professional and life skills, and a commitment to their work in supporting the children who attend the centre. Trustees should reconsider their cycle of review to ensure adherence to legal requirements.

Key Next Steps

Centre leaders agree that their next steps are to access professional development to help teachers to:

  • review the quality of planning, evaluation and assessment with an emphasis on children's individual learning journeys

  • extend children's learning through the use of meaningful interactions.

Recommendation

ERO recommends that trustees access external advisory support to develop an effective appraisal management system.

Management Assurance on Legal Requirements

Before the review, the staff and management of Tamariki Takitumu Punanga Reo completed an ERO Centre Assurance Statement and Self-Audit Checklist. In these documents they attested that they have taken all reasonable steps to meet their legal obligations related to:

  • curriculum
  • premises and facilities
  • health and safety practices
  • governance, management and administration.

During the review, ERO looked at the service‚Äôs systems for managing the following areas that have a potentially high impact on children's wellbeing:

  • emotional safety (including positive guidance and child protection)

  • physical safety (including supervision; sleep procedures; accidents; medication; hygiene; excursion policies and procedures)

  • suitable staffing (including qualification levels; police vetting; teacher registration; ratios)

  • evacuation procedures and practices for fire and earthquake.

All early childhood services are required to promote children's health and safety and to regularly review their compliance with legal requirements.

To improve current practice, the early childhood service management should review their policies and procedures to ensure they meet all current legal requirements.

ERO identified an area of non-compliance. In order to address this, managers must implement a system of regular performance appraisal that meets the requirements of the Education Council of NZ.
