Lollipops Educare Waikato Hospital - 30/07/2013

  • On this page:

  • 1 Evaluation Findings
  • 2 Information about the Service

1 Evaluation Findings

Background

Lollipops Educare Waikato Hospital is one of two early childhood education and care centres in Hamilton operating under the management of Lollipops Educare Limited New Zealand. It is located close to the central city, adjacent to the Waikato Hospital. It is licensed for 50 children, including up to 25 children less than two years of age. Most teachers are qualified early childhood teachers, and others are in training. A new centre director, a qualified early childhood teacher, was appointed recently. Since the last ERO review, there have been substantial changes in the leadership structure and to staffing.

The centre director, together with the national centre support manager, has made significant progress in improving the centre culture and quality of education since the 2012 ERO review. She is fostering links with hospital kaumātua and chaplain, teachers from the other Lollipops centre, and the wider community.

The centre is well-supported. Advice and guidance from the national office, as well as the appointment of a centre support worker, have underpinned the growth of the service. Sound progress has been made in a short period of time. 

Areas of progress

Improvements to the environment have been a priority for national and centre managers. They organised structural changes to the physical environment that have controlled noise levels and improved children’s access to play areas and resources. Babies and toddlers enjoy a comfortable, attractive, and nurturing environment. They are now able to move more freely to visit friends and siblings in the older children’s area. They also have greater opportunities to explore the natural and outdoor environment. The learning areas for older children are organised to offer a wide choice of activities and experiences and promote independent learning. The addition of blinds and shade sails allows children to use play areas throughout the year. Teachers’ increased responsibility for purchasing resources for learning has enabled them to select equipment and materials that encourage children’s exploration and their involvement in learning.

Purposeful, experienced and well-informed leadership has built a climate of teamwork, trust and open communication. Team leaders and teachers are being empowered to contribute to decision making and take on leadership responsibilities. Relationships among teachers, parents, whānau and children are inclusive, open, respectful and affirming. Increased emphasis on te ao Māori is evident in the programme, environment, and teacher practice. These initiatives are supporting the sustainability of recent developments.

A review of teaching philosophy is promoting teachers’ in-depth reflection on the centre’s vision, principles of learning, and priorities for the learning programme. Planning and assessment processes have been reviewed and improved. They are being used effectively to notice, recognise and respond to children’s interests, ideas, and the aspirations of parents and whānau. Children’s learning is visible in classroom displays and in children’s individual portfolios. Teachers plan and work together using each other’s strengths, their knowledge of children, and professional understandings. Significant whole-centre professional development is assisting teachers to develop shared views about best practice in early childhood education.

Management has given careful attention to recruiting staff members who have appropriate skills and values that reflect the centre’s philosophy and vision. New staff members are given well-structured induction to their roles. Appraisal processes are well established. They include an emphasis on centre and individual staff goals, and teachers are keeping reflective journals as a tool to enhance their capabilities.

Self review is contributing to improvements to teacher practice and centre operations. Teachers and leaders are involved in spontaneous and planned reviews. The process of reviews about learning is highly visible, as information is displayed on centre walls. Research is used to identify indicators for success, and evidence is collected from a variety of sources and reflects a range of voices. Findings from self review, as well as from the 2012 ERO report, contribute to strategic priorities and action plans for improvement.

Areas for further improvement

The review of planning and assessment has resulted in teachers responding more effectively to support and extend children’s learning. Whole-centre professional development has the potential to further enhance teachers’ implementation of best practice in planning and assessment processes. This could include a continuing focus on:

  • analysing the learning to identify children’s developing skills, knowledge and patterns of learning
  • encouraging parents’ voices
  • increasing the records of children’s voices in individual portfolios.

Next ERO Review

When is ERO likely to review the service again?

The next ERO review of Lollipops Educare Waikato Hospital will be in three years.
