1 Evaluation of Opotiki Rose Garden Preschool

How well placed is Opotiki Rose Garden Preschool to promote positive learning outcomes for children?

Well placed

Very well placed

ERO's findings that support this overall judgement are summarised below.

Background

Opotiki Rose Garden Preschool is a community-based early childhood service. It provides all-day education and care for children from birth to school age in mixed-age sessions. The centre is licensed to cater for up to 35 children, including 6 children up to the age of two years. At the time of this ERO review 45 children were attending, of whom 22 are identified as MƒÅori. Children learn together in a mixed-age group setting.

The service operates under a charitable trust of elected parent members. The philosophy states that the service values and honours each unique child as learners through authentic, play-based experiences. It acknowledges the importance of MƒÅori as tangata whenua, and aims to reflect the cultural heritage of all children, whƒÅnau and their community.

An acting head teacher/manager has recently been appointed. She is supported by very experienced, qualified teachers, including one teacher awaiting registration.

The centre has a positive reporting history with ERO. Since the 2013 ERO review the centre has continued to undertake professional development that has improved teacher understanding and practice in planning, assessing and evaluating the curriculum to reflect the intentions of their philosophy.

The Review Findings

Children enjoy playing and learning in an environment that has been designed and developed to support their learning and growing. Particular strengths observed by ERO were the high levels of physical activity, and the opportunities for children to tend gardens and harvest fruit and vegetables, which they help to cook to provide healthy food. Social interactions, and a high level of oral communication are evident between individuals and among groups of children.

Children also explore rich opportunities that build their skills and knowledge of early literacy, mathematics and science as natural aspects of the daily programme. ERO observed children intently involved in construction using a variety of quality equipment, listening to stories, and participating in music, movement and group sharing. Te reo and tikanga MƒÅori are included in the programme as teachers include a MƒÅori perspective in conversations, and through waiata, karakia and MƒÅori legends. The identity and language of children from other ethnicities are also respected and acknowledged.

Children aged up to two years benefit from learning and developing in a calm and relaxed learning environment. They enjoy playing alongside older children, including their siblings. A separate sleep room has recently been provided, ensuring that children's individual sleep rhythms and home routines can be respected.

A review of the daily programme has ensured that children have more time for sustained play and exploration. By limiting the expectations for all children to gather for a group morning tea and mat time, teachers are able to respect children's own choices and help them develop their self-management skills. Children and teachers eat together at lunch time, sharing social routines, emphasising healthy food and nutrition.

Teachers have positive and responsive relationships with children, and know them well. Parents and whƒÅnau are welcome to spend time in the centre, and state that they appreciate the opportunity to talk with teachers and share children's home experiences. Teachers are very aware of children's interests and preferences, as well as their parents' aspirations. Teachers skilfully support and extend children's learning through conversations, open questions and working alongside them to develop their thinking and experimenting.

Teachers are competent, experienced and work collaboratively to plan the programme in response to children's interests and to build on their learning. They draw on a wide range of good quality equipment and resources, and also make effective use of the wider local environment. This adds breadth and depth to the programme, and involves children in community activities. Teachers have developed useful systems to identify children's learning and share it with parents and children. Internet-based learning portfolios enable parents, and whƒÅnau from anywhere in the world, to read and respond to entries that tell them about their children's interests and activities. The centre welcomes parents to stay as they help their children to settle into the centre. A new approach has been to provide parents with information that aligns their child's early learning with the New Zealand school curriculum to assist their transition to school.

The current head teacher/manager, who was recently appointed to relieve in this position contributes to the open, transparent and collaborative culture of the centre. She has initiated a variety of self-review topics that have assisted trustees to consider improvements to the organisation's policy and practice. Other reviews have introduced new possibilities for teachers to take on responsibilities. Teachers appreciate the opportunities to be meaningfully involved and contribute to strategic planning and curriculum development.

The management trust is composed of elected parents and whƒÅnau and provides oversight of the centre's operations, finance and property. There has been an ongoing initiative of renovation and refurbishment which still continues, and has contributed significantly to the quality and usefulness of the centre facilities. In particular, the kitchen is better equipped and more accessible and adds to the home-like quality of the environment. The trust supports teacher professional development which is contributing to the inclusion of new practices and skills to the centre programme.

Key Next Steps

Key next steps for centre leaders are to:

  • review assessment processes to more consistently identify progress in children's learning and development over time

  • consider re-introducing printed portfolios alongside the current digital versions. This would allow children to revisit their learning more often, and benefit from using it as a valuable, personalised reading resource.

Leaders also need to continue to develop centre appraisal process to include emphasis on teacher reflections and self-evaluations, and associated feedback and feedforward about how they are improving their practice over time.

Management Assurance on Legal Requirements

Before the review, the staff and management of Opotiki Rose Garden Preschool completed an ERO Centre Assurance Statement and Self-Audit Checklist. In these documents they attested that they have taken all reasonable steps to meet their legal obligations related to:

  • curriculum

  • premises and facilities

  • health and safety practices

  • governance, management and administration.

During the review, ERO looked at the service‚Äôs systems for managing the following areas that have a potentially high impact on children's wellbeing:

  • emotional safety (including positive guidance and child protection)

  • physical safety (including supervision; sleep procedures; accidents; medication; hygiene; excursion policies and procedures)

  • suitable staffing (including qualification levels; police vetting; teacher registration; ratios)

  • evacuation procedures and practices for fire and earthquake.

All early childhood services are required to promote children's health and safety and to regularly review their compliance with legal requirements.

ERO identified an area of non-compliance.

The service provider must implement suitable human resource practices. Processes for human resource management must include appropriate selection and appointment practices.[Licensing Criteria for Early Childhood Education and Care Centres 2008, GMA7]

Next ERO Review

When is ERO likely to review the service again?

The next ERO review of Opotiki Rose Garden Preschool will be in three years.
