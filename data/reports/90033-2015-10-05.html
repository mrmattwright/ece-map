1 Te Aromātai I te Ratonga

He pēhea te tūnga o Te Rourou Whakatipuranga o Awarua ki te whakatairanga i te pai o ngā putanga ako mō ngā tamariki?

Kāhore i te pai te tūnga

Me whakawhanake ake

He pai te tūnga

He tino pai te tūnga

Ko ngā kitenga a Te Tari Arotake Mātauranga e hāpai ana i te whānuitanga o tēnei whakataunga, kua whakarāpopotongia ki raro iho nei.

He Whakamārama

E whakakaurera ana Te Rourou Whakatipuranga o Awarua i te whanga o Motu-Pōhue, ā, e tū ana ki te marae o Te Rau Aroha ki Awarua. E tino tautokona ana te whare kōhungahunga nei e te Rūnaka o Awarua, Te Rūnaka o Kāi Tahu, ngā kaitiaki o te marae, te rōpū whakahaere o te whare kōhungahunga, me te whānau. Nō te marae tonu te nuinga o ngā whānau me ngā kaimahi.

Ka noho pūmau, ka noho ngākau nui hoki te hapori o te whare kōhungahunga ki te whakaratonga o ngā hōtaka akoranga whakaongaonga hei whakawhānui ake hoki i te tūmāia me te ako a ngā tamariki. He tino māramatanga tō ngā kaiārahi, ngā kaiako me ngā kaitiaki o te whare kōhungahunga ki tā rātou rapunga whakaaro e pā ana ki ngā tamariki me te whare kōhungahunga. Ka hāngai te tirohanga me te rapunga whakaaro a te whare kōhungahunga ki ngā wawata o te marae me te whānau. Ka whakarato te whare kōhungahunga i tētahi marautanga ā-hapori e ahu mai ana i te ao Māori, ā, e whakakoia ana i ngā taonga tuku iho a ngā tamariki me tō rātou reo.

Mai i te arotake o mua a Te Tari Arotake Mātauranga, kua whakatutukihia e te whānau ētahi tino wero i pupū ake ai nā te whakariterite anō i ngā pūnaha e pā ana ki ngā kaimahi me te whakahaeretanga.

Ngā Whakaaturanga o te Arotake

Ka rongo ngā tamariki i te manaaki me te whakaute ki ō rātou hononga. Ko ngā whakawhitinga kōrero me ngā taunekeneke pūmau ki waenga i ngā kaimahi me ngā tamariki e whakaongaonga ana. Ka whai ngā kaimahi i ngā ngākau nuitanga o ngā tamariki, ā, ka whakawhānui i ō rātou whakaaro me tō rātou reo. Ka ākina ngā hononga o te tuakana me te teina ki waenga i ngā tamariki. Ka whakatauira ngā kaimahi i te whanaungatanga, te aroha, te manaaki, me te tiaki. He harikoa, he tau hoki ngā tamariki i roto i tō rātou taiao manaaki.

Ka whakamanahia, ka whakakoia hoki te aronga toi whenuatanga o ngā tamariki me tō rātou tuakiri mā te marautanga ā-hapori. Ka whakatairanga te hōtaka akoranga i te tūhura o te ao māoriori, tūturu hoki. Ka ako ngā tamariki i tō rātou ahurea me ā rātou taonga tuku iho. Ko te wharenui tētahi tino puna ako, ā, ka ako ngā tamariki i ō rātou tikanga me ō rātou tūpuna i reira. Ka waiata ngā tamariki, ka taki hoki rātou i ō rātou pepeha, ā, ka whakanui ēnei i ō rātou hononga ki tō rātou marae me tō rātou iwi. Ka whai wāhi ngā tamariki ki te kohinga o ngā tītī i ia tau, ā, he kaupapa nui tēnei ki tō rātou hapori. Ka whai wāhi ngā tamariki ki ngā tikanga tuku iho o ō rātou tūpuna.

Ka ako ngā tamariki i te reo Māori ki te whānuitanga o ngā tūmomo horopaki. Ka whakapuaki rātou i tō rātou reo Māori ki te marae, ki te taha o ō rātou whānau, ā, i a rātou e tūhura ana hoki i te hapori whānui. Ka hāpai aua whai wāhitanga i te whakawhanaketanga o ā rātou kupu kōrero. Kei te tīmata ngā tamariki ki āta kōrero i te reo Māori.

He pārekareka te ako ki ngā tamariki. Ka whakaongaonga te hōtaka, ā, ka whakahāngaitia hoki hei poipoi i te puāwaitanga o ngā ngākau nuitanga o ngā tamariki. Ka whai wā ngā tamariki ki te hoki atu anō ki ngā taumahi, me te tākaro mō te wā roa. He whai wāhitanga mō ngā tamariki ki te whakatōpū i tēnā e mōhiotia ana e rātou, me te whakahōhonu ake hoki i ō rātou mōhiotanga. E wātea ana ki ngā tamariki te whānuitanga o ngā rauemi me ngā wāhi akoranga o roto, o waho hoki. He pakari te whakawhitiwhiti haere a ngā tamariki puta noa i te whare kōhungahunga. Ka tino tautokona rātou kia kōkiri ake ai rātou i ā rātou akoranga.

Ka kitea te whakaute o ngā tamariki mō tō rātou wāhi, ā, ka manaakitia e rātou. Kua āta waihangatia te taiao ako hei hāpai i ngā akoranga a ngā tamariki. He maha ngā wāhi i whakaritea ai mō te hanganga, te kori tinana, me te āta whakapā atu ki te whenua. E āhei ana ngā tamariki ki raro i te rua o ngā tau te tūhura i te taiao mā ō rātou tairongo katoa. E whakawhanake ana ngā tamariki hei kaitiaki o tō rātou taiao.

E tino tautokona ana te mātauranga, te atawhai hoki o ngā tamariki e ngā kaimahi e tino ngākau nuitia ana, e tino whai tohu ana hoki. Ka arotahi ngā kaimahi ki te whakarato i ngā hōtaka akoranga e aro ana ki ngā matea o te katoa o te tamaiti. Ko te whakawhanaketanga o ia tamaiti, ka āta aroturukitia, ka āta aromātaitia hoki e ngā kaimahi. Ka āta whakaarohia te whānuitanga o ngā matea ako o ngā tamariki. I ngā wā katoa, ka rapu tonu ngā kaimahi i ngā huarahi ka aro nui ki te rerekē haere o ngā matea o ngā tamariki.

Ka ngana ngā tamariki ki te ako. I pai ake te marautanga nā te whai huatanga o te arotake whaiaro ia te wā. Ka whai hua ngā kaimahi ki te whakawhanake mārika i te whakaaro huritao, te arotake ngaio hoki, me te aronga o tēnei ki ngā matea me ngā wawata o ngā tamariki me te whānau. Ka tāutu rātou i ngā wāhanga hei whakatupu ake, hei whakapai ake hoki. I tēnei wā kei te arotake, kei te whakahou ake hoki ngā kaimahi i ngā kaupapa here me ngā tukanga, kia hāngai ai ēnei ki te rapunga whakaaro o te whare kōhungahunga i whakahoungia ai.

Ngā Whakaritenga Matua ka Whai Ake

E mōhio ana ngā kaiwhakahaere o te whare kōhungahunga, e tika ana kia whai rātou i tētahi rautaki ka whakapiki ake i te whakamahinga a ngā kaimahi i te reo Māori. Ko te kōkiri ake i te whakamahinga o te reo Māori ki waenga i ngā kaimahi me ngā tamariki tētahi kaupapa matua.

Me whakapakari ake i ētahi āhuatanga o te arotake whaiaro. Ka hāpai tēnei i te whakapūmautanga o te āhua mahi pai, ā, ka whakatairanga hoki i te tupu haere tonu, puta noa i te whare kōhungahunga.

Te Whakataunga a te Whakahaeretanga ki ngā Wāhanga Tautukunga

I te wā o te arotake, i whakamātauhia e te Tari Arotake Mātauranga ngā pūnaha o te ratonga ki te whakahaere i ngā āhuatanga i raro iho nei e whai pānga nui ana ki te waiora o ngā tamariki:

  • te haumaru whatumanawa (tāpiri atu ko te ārahi mauritau, ko te ārai tamariki)
  • te haumaru ā-tinana (tāpiri atu ko te mātakitaki tamariki; ko ngā whakaritenga whakamoe; ko ngā aituā; ko te whāngai rongoā; ko ngā whakaritenga akuaku; ko ngā kaupapa here me ngā tikanga haerenga whakawaho)
  • te tū tika o ngā kaimahi (tae atu ki ngā taumata tohu mātauranga; te arowhai a ngā pirihimana; ngā rēhitatanga kaiako; me te tatauranga ki waenga i te kaiako me te tamaiti)
  • ngā whakaritenga, tikanga hōneatanga e pā ana ki te ahi me te rū whenua.

Ko te tikanga, ka whakatairanga ngā ratonga mātauranga kōhungahunga katoa i te hauora me te haumaru o ngā tamariki, me te arotake anō hoki i ia te wā i tā rātou whakatutukitanga i ngā herenga ā-ture.

1 Evaluation of the Service

Well placed

Very well placed

ERO's findings that support this overall judgement are summarised below.

Background

Te Rourou Whakatipuranga o Awarua overlooks Bluff Harbour and is at Te Rau Aroha Marae, Awarua. The centre is well supported by the Awarua Rūnaka, Te Rūnaka o Kāi Tahu, marae trustees, centre management and whānau. Most whānau and kaimahi affiliate to the marae.

The centre community is dedicated and passionate about providing stimulating learning programmes to build and extend children’s confidence and learning. Centre leaders, teachers and trustees have a clear understanding about their philosophy for the children and centre. The centre vision and philosophy aligns to the marae and whānau aspirations. The centre provides a local curriculum that is based on te ao Māori and affirms children’s heritage and language.

Since the previous ERO review, whānau addressed significant challenges that resulted in the reorganisation of staffing and management systems.

The Review Findings

Children experience caring and respectful relationships. Conversations and sustained interactions between kaimahi and children are stimulating. Kaimahi follow children’s interests and extend their thinking and language. Tuakana and teina relationships are encouraged among children. Kaimahi model whanaungatanga, aroha, manaaki and tiaki. Children are happy and settled in their caring environment.

Children’s sense of belonging and identity is valued and affirmed through the local curriculum. The learning programme promotes the exploration of the natural and physical worlds. Children learn about their culture and heritage. The wharenui is an important context for learning where children learn about their tikanga and tūpuna. Children sing waiata and recite their pepeha which acknowledges their connections to their marae and iwi. Children take part in the annual gathering of tītī which is significant to their community. Children participate in traditional practices of their tūpuna.

Children learn te reo Māori in a range of contexts. They practice using te reo Māori on the marae, with their whānau and as they explore the wider community. These opportunities support the development of vocabulary. Children are at the early stages of becoming te reo Māori speakers.

Children enjoy learning. The programme is flexible and adapted to meet the emerging interests of children. Children have time to revisit activities and play for sustained periods of time. Children have opportunities to consolidate what they know and deepen their understanding. Children have access to a range of resources and a vast array of learning spaces indoors and outside. Children move confidently throughout the centre. They are well supported to develop as independent learners.

Children respect and care for their surroundings. The learning environment is carefully designed to support children’s learning. There are several spaces for construction, physical play and hands on interaction with the land. The under-two year olds are able to explore the environment using all of their senses. Children are developing as kaitiaki for their environment.

Children are well supported in their education and care by dedicated, qualified staff. Staff focus on providing learning programmes that respond to the holistic needs of children. Each child’s development is closely monitored and evaluated by staff. The diverse learning needs of children are well considered. Staff continually seek ways to respond to the changing needs of children.

Children are engaged learners. The curriculum improved as a result of ongoing self-review. Staff have developed a culture of professional reflection and review that focuses on the needs and aspirations of children and whānau. They identify areas for growth and improvement. Staff are currently reviewing and updating policies, procedures to align to the revised centre philosophy.

Key Next Steps

Centre managers recognise the need for a strategy to increase staff use of te reo Māori. Acceleration of te reo Māori use among staff and children is a priority.

There is a need to strengthen some aspects of self-review. This will help to sustain good performance and promote continual growth throughout the centre.

Management Assurance on Legal Requirements

During the review, ERO looked at the service’s systems for managing the following areas that have a potentially high impact on children's wellbeing:

  • emotional safety (including positive guidance and child protection)
  • physical safety (including supervision; sleep procedures; accidents; medication; hygiene; excursion policies and procedures)
  • suitable staffing (including qualification levels; police vetting; teacher registration; ratios)
  • evacuation procedures and practices for fire and earthquake.

All early childhood services are required to promote children's health and safety and to regularly review their compliance with legal requirements.

Next Review

When is ERO likely to review the service again?

The next ERO review of Te Rourou Whakatipuranga o Awarua will be in three years.

3 General Information about Early Childhood Reviews

ERO’s Evaluation Framework

ERO’s overarching question for an early childhood education review is ‘How well placed is this service to promote positive learning outcomes for children?’ ERO focuses on the following factors as described in the bicultural framework Ngā Pou Here:

  • Pou Whakahaere – how the service determines its vision, philosophy and direction to ensure positive outcomes for children
  • Pou Ārahi – how leadership is enacted to enhance positive outcomes for children
  • Mātauranga – whose knowledge is valued and how the curriculum is designed to achieve positive outcomes for children
  • Tikanga whakaako – how approaches to teaching and learning respond to diversity and support positive outcomes for children.

Within these areas ERO considers the effectiveness of arotake – self review and of whanaungatanga – partnerships with parents and whānau.

ERO evaluates how well placed a service is to sustain good practice and make ongoing improvements for the benefit of all children at the service.

A focus for the government is that all children, especially priority learners, have an opportunity to benefit from quality early childhood education. ERO will report on how well each service promotes positive outcomes for all children, with a focus on children who are Māori, Pacific, have diverse needs, and are up to the age of two.

For more information about the framework and Ngā Pou Here refer to ERO’s Approach to Review in Early Childhood Services.

ERO’s Overall Judgement and Next Review

The overall judgement that ERO makes and the timing of the next review will depend on how well placed a service is to promote positive learning outcomes for children. The categories are:

  • Very well placed – The next ERO review in four years
  • Well placed – The next ERO review in three years
  • Requires further development – The next ERO review within two years
  • Not well placed - The next ERO review in consultation with the Ministry of Education

ERO has developed criteria for each category. These are available on ERO’s website.

Review Coverage

ERO reviews are tailored to each service’s context and performance, within the overarching review framework. The aim is to provide information on aspects that are central to positive outcomes for children and useful to the service.
