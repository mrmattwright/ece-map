Background

Park Road Playcentre is one of 19 administered by the Central Districts Playcentre Association (the association). This review is one of ten undertaken by ERO in the association‚Äôs playcentres during Term 4, 2014.

The centre is open for five mornings per week and caters for children from birth to six years of age. Responsibility for day-to-day operations is undertaken by centre-elected office holders. A paid team leader supports parents and whƒÅnau to develop and implement the daily programme. Professional advice and feedback to strengthen members‚Äô practice is provided by a liaison officer employed by the association.

Since the June 2012 ERO report, the centre has had several changes of team leader. At the time of this review, an acting team leader is supporting members. A high proportion of parents have gained course two, or above, Playcentre qualifications.

Parents come from a wide geographical area and represent a range of ethnicities and cultures. Many families have connections with the nearby military camp and tertiary institutions, so there is a regular turnover of members.

The Review Findings

The philosophy of the centre is well reflected in practice. Adults view children as competent and capable of leading their own learning. Experiences are planned to effectively promote children‚Äôs self management, independence and creative self expression. Children are confident, cooperative and friendly.

Parents and whƒÅnau work well as a team to engage children‚Äôs interest and involvement in activities. Their strengths and interests are well known by all.

Further development strategies that members can use to extend children‚Äôs ideas and understanding should be a focus for improved practice.

The environment provides a variety of learning experiences for children. Carefully organised resources are freely available. An interesting outdoor area provides children with opportunities for challenge, inquiry and physical activities. Nearby parks and gardens are well used for excursions.

Further provision of opportunities for children to see their work and reflect on their achievements is an appropriate development step.

Inclusive centre practices support families with children with diverse needs, cultural backgrounds and aspirations. Good use of local agencies promotes participation of children with special needs. Families‚Äô cultural practices are shared and celebrated.

Infants and toddlers fully participate in sessions and learn alongside older children. A suitable range of sensory materials and equipment caters for their use.

Children‚Äôs developing interests are regularly discussed and recorded. Daily evaluation meetings are used for reflection and planning. Regular meetings provide opportunities for collaborative reflection and for parents to share their aspirations for their children.

Increased participation of parents in assessment and planning for learning is actively supported by the core group of more experienced members. Further development of children‚Äôs use of mathematical language and ideas should improve their confidence as mathematical learners.

Strengthening children‚Äôs transition to school is an area of ongoing development.

Strong and committed leadership by a core group of members is promoting sustained good practice. Mutual support and an emphasis on relationship-building is fostering parents‚Äô confidence and willingness to become involved.

Self review is valued as a process to promote improvement. Collaborative reviews of many aspects of practice are evident. The strategic plan and actions outline a clear direction for centre development. Further development of more evaluative review based on good practice indicators to inform decisions is a next step.

Useful support is provided by the association. This includes written guidelines and systems for managing finance and legislative obligations. Work is being completed to improve employment practices. Regular visits from the liaison officer assist members in their management and teaching roles. A review of the liaison officer role is being undertaken to support an improved approach.

The appraisal process, particularly for those employed as team leaders and liaison officers, needs further development. The provision of ongoing professional, constructive feedback based on observations of practice, and linked to identified needs and development goals, should add rigour.

Management Assurance on Legal Requirements

Before the review, the staff and management of Park Road Playcentre completed an ERO Centre Assurance Statement and Self-Audit Checklist. In these documents they attested that they have taken all reasonable steps to meet their legal obligations related to:

  • curriculum
  • premises and facilities
  • health and safety practices
  • governance, management and administration.

During the review, ERO looked at the service‚Äôs systems for managing the following areas that have a potentially high impact on children's wellbeing:

  • emotional safety (including positive guidance and child protection)
  • physical safety (including supervision; sleep procedures; accidents; medication; hygiene; excursion policies and procedures)
  • suitable staffing (including qualification levels; police vetting; teacher registration; ratios)
  • evacuation procedures and practices for fire and earthquake.

All early childhood services are required to promote children's health and safety and to regularly review their compliance with legal requirements.
