1 Evaluation of The Cubbyhouse Early Childhood Centre

ERO's findings that support this overall judgement are summarised below.

Background

The Cubbyhouse Early Childhood Centre is one of a cluster of four private early childhood centres under common ownership. It is licensed for a maximum of 110 children, including 40 aged up to two years. Of the 142 children enrolled, 18 identify as MƒÅori. Children learn in four separate rooms with shared areas.

The two owners/directors have oversight of personnel and financial management. Responsibility for day-to-day operation and curriculum implementation is delegated to a manager. Each classroom is overseen by a team leader. Of the 22 permanently kaiako (teachers) employed, 14 are registered.

The philosophy underpinning teaching and learning emphasises the importance of relationships, acknowledgement of New Zealand‚Äôs dual cultural heritage, creativity and child-initiated play.

The January 2015 ERO report identified the need to further develop: processes to build the quality of teaching and learning, including the appraisal process; the bicultural programme and ways to work with MƒÅori learners; self review; assessment; and annual planning.

This review is one of a cluster of four in the Cubbyhouse umbrella organisation.

The Review Findings

The curriculum strongly reflects the values outlined in the centre's philosophy statement. Children are active, engaged learners who are well supported by kaiako to settle into and persevere in their play. Trusting relationships are evident with their kaiako. The teaching team has an enduring focus on the development of partnerships with parents and whƒÅnau to strengthen support for children's learning.

Kaiako are working to implement practices that promote children's independence, self management and empowerment as learners. The outdoor environment is carefully planned to encourage exploration and challenge. Routines are flexible and children are offered choices about decisions that affect them. Resources are presented to promote their interest, participation and creative self-expression. Children display strong ownership of the environment.

Provision for infants is well developed. Consistent caregiving supports secure and trusting relationships between children and adults. Key kaiako are attuned to children's cues and effectively promote their sustained engagement in play and learning. Infants display a strong sense of belonging and wellbeing. They are confident and independent learners.

A thoughtful approach has been taken to the development of a bicultural perspective in the programme, and of practices that should support MƒÅori children's success as MƒÅori. A range of research and publications have been considered. Kaiako have recently incorporated MƒÅori values in children's learning summaries, and acknowledgment of te ao MƒÅori in internal evaluation and planning processes. Aspects of tikanga are a regular part of the daily programme. Links are being developed with whƒÅnau MƒÅori to support the team's approach.

Children requiring additional learning support are identified and assisted to participate fully in the programme. Planning processes consider the impact of the programme on these learners. Kaiako work collaboratively with outside agencies, parents and whƒÅnau to identify and progress children's learning goals.

Children‚Äôs transitions into and through the service are carefully considered. Kaiako and families work together to plan an approach that maintains children's relationships and promotes their wellbeing.

Support is provided to assist children's transition to school. Information about local schools is made available to parents. Relationships between centre staff and some local school personnel are developing. Leaders should continue to seek ways to share information with school personnel to support the continuity of children‚Äôs learning.

A comprehensive approach to planning the programme is in place, linked to children's interests and parents' and whƒÅnau aspirations. Use of an online program supports improved communication between kaiako and families about aspects of children's learning. Assessment records show that some kaiako are putting in place strategies to support the progress of individual children over time. The team should: work on maximising the focus on outcomes for individual children to effectively support decision making about next learning/teaching steps; and continue to seek ways of supporting children's timely and ongoing reflection on their learning and achievement.

A well-established approach to review and development is in place. Inquiry and research into aspects of practice are undertaken by all kaiako. Reflection and collaboration are strong features of the team's approach. Understanding and use of internal evaluation is developing. A suitable framework has been adopted. Its effective use, including identification of indicators of high quality to support decision making about development and collection of relevant evidence, is a next step.

A collaborative, collegial team culture is in place, supported by distributed leadership opportunities. The manager has an inclusive and improvement-focused approach clearly centred on children and sound philosophical values linked to teaching and learning.

Good support is available for kaiako and leaders to develop their practice. The appraisal process continues to evolve. The manager has made plans to ensure:

  • regular, formal observations of practice are undertaken in relation to kaiako goals

  • evidence of kaiako practice is relevant and clearly demonstrates progress towards goals and teaching standards.

Some well-aligned governance and management processes support improvement in practice and operation over time. Regular management reports provides assurance that expectations for operation are being met. Leaders work collaboratively to identify and progress strategic priorities. Next steps at governance level are to:

  • review the overarching Cubbyhouse philosophy

  • establish a planned approach to implementation of a bicultural perspective across the Cubbyhouse organisation

  • further develop the strategic framework to better define goals, actions and desired outcomes, and strengthen alignment with teacher development and planned internal evaluation.

Key Next Steps

ERO, the director and manager agree the priority at centre level is to continue to strengthen:

  • planning for learning.

At governance level priorities are to support development of

  • a strategic approach to implementing Te Tiriti-based practice

  • understanding and use of internal evaluation at all levels

  • the teacher appraisal process

  • an improved strategic planning framework to promote sustainability of operation.

Management Assurance on Legal Requirements

Before the review, the staff and management of The Cubbyhouse Early Childhood Centre completed an ERO Centre Assurance Statement and Self-Audit Checklist. In these documents they attested that they have taken all reasonable steps to meet their legal obligations related to:

  • curriculum
  • premises and facilities
  • health and safety practices
  • governance, management and administration.

During the review, ERO looked at the service‚Äôs systems for managing the following areas that have a potentially high impact on children's wellbeing:

  • emotional safety (including positive guidance and child protection)

  • physical safety (including supervision; sleep procedures; accidents; medication; hygiene; excursion policies and procedures)

  • suitable staffing (including qualification levels; police vetting; teacher registration; ratios)

  • evacuation procedures and practices for fire and earthquake.

All early childhood services are required to promote children's health and safety and to regularly review their compliance with legal requirements.

When is ERO likely to review the service again?

The next ERO review of The Cubbyhouse Early Childhood Centre will be in three years.

ERO‚Äôs Evaluation Framework

ERO‚Äôs overarching question for an early childhood education review is ‚ÄòHow well placed is this service to promote positive learning outcomes for children?‚Äô ERO focuses on the following factors as described in the bicultural framework NgƒÅ Pou Here:

ERO evaluates how well placed a service is to sustain good practice and make ongoing improvements for the benefit of all children at the service.

A focus for the government is that all children, especially priority learners, have an opportunity to benefit from quality early childhood education. ERO will report on how well each service promotes positive outcomes for all children, with a focus on children who are MƒÅori, Pacific, have diverse needs, and are up to the age of two.

ERO‚Äôs Overall Judgement and Next Review

The overall judgement that ERO makes and the timing of the next review will depend on how well placed a service is to promote positive learning outcomes for children. The categories are:

  • Well placed ‚Äì The next ERO review in three years
  • Requires further development ‚Äì The next ERO review within two years
  • Not well placed - The next ERO review in consultation with the Ministry of Education

ERO has developed criteria for each category. These are available on ERO‚Äôs website.

Review Coverage

ERO reviews are tailored to each service‚Äôs context and performance, within the overarching review framework. The aim is to provide information on aspects that are central to positive outcomes for children and useful to the service.
