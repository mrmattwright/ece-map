ERO's findings that support this overall judgement are summarised below.

Background

Frimley Early Learning Centre is situated at Hastings. It provides all day education and care for children from birth to school age, five days a week. At the time of this ERO review, 18 of the children enrolled identified as MƒÅori and two are of Pacific heritage.

The service philosophy focuses on building strong relationships and positive wairua to develop children‚Äôs independence and competence.

Since the previous ERO review there have been several changes in leadership. The centre manager oversees the day-to-day operation of the centre and supports the teaching team. Most teachers are qualified. They represent a diverse range of cultures.

The February 2016 ERO report identified areas requiring further improvement. These included developing and embedding assessment and planning practices and strengthening the evaluative aspect of self review. Good progress has been made to address these areas.

The centre is owned and operated by Provincial Education Group Ltd (the organisation). It provides governance and management support. A substantial management team oversees a number of services nationwide. This includes a professional leader who provides support and guidance to the teaching team. Te Tiriti o Waitangi principles are central to the organisation's philosophy and practices.

Since the previous ERO review, the indoor and outdoor spaces have undergone significant development to provide additional learning opportunities for children.

The Review Findings

Children lead their own learning within a curriculum that is responsive to their interests and developing dispositions. Redeveloped spaces encourage children to explore, engage and have fun in play for sustained periods of time.

Teachers are responsive to children‚Äôs cues, prompts and questions. They use a range of strategies to develop children‚Äôs problem-solving and social competence. Warm, respectful relationships are modelled throughout the centre.

Infants and toddlers experience a calm and unhurried learning environment. Teachers offer them choices about what is to happen and wait for them to respond. Children are familiar with the routines of the room and demonstrate a sense of belonging.

Aspects of te ao MƒÅori are evident in the programme. Specific resources and events provide opportunities to experience kaupapa MƒÅori concepts. Some teachers use te reo MƒÅori in general conversations with children. Encouraging greater use of te reo MƒÅori in everyday practice is a key next step. In addition, leaders and teachers should continue to grow their understanding of places of significant value for MƒÅori and celebrate these within the curriculum.

Assessment, planning and evaluation of children‚Äôs learning is currently being strengthened. These improvements should show that teachers are collaboratively developing learning goals with parents and whƒÅnau and more clearly identifying children's learning over time. Teachers are beginning to work with parents and whƒÅnau to build a comprehensive picture of who each child is, their strengths and what they bring to their learning. As this process continues to develop, leaders and teachers should consider how they can:

  • consistently make children‚Äôs culture, language and identity more visible in their learning

  • draw from the aspirations of whƒÅnau MƒÅori and Pacific community to determine what educational success looks like for them in this context.

Teachers have a well-considered approach to support children and their whƒÅnau when transitioning to school. Older children experience a programme aimed at developing self-confidence and competence skills. They have regularly opportunities to experience a school environment.

Leaders and teachers are beginning to implement a useful internal evaluation framework. This should be strengthened by developing relevant indicators of high-quality practice to support evaluation and decision making.

Provincial Education Group senior leadership has high expectations for teacher practice. Systems and processes are in place to promote improvements, for the benefit of children and whƒÅnau. Teachers are encouraged to grow their understanding and take on distributed leadership roles.

A sound appraisal framework is in place that leads teachers to investigate aspects of their practice and make ongoing improvements.

A well-considered policy framework is in place to guide teacher practice. The organisation places considerable emphasis on the health, safety and wellbeing of children. A formal reporting process is now being implemented to provide feedback to teachers about the focus of professional leaders' visits.

Parent surveys have affirmed teacher practice and provided feedback for ongoing consideration. Leaders have been pro-active in responding to the survey findings.

A recent national initiative, led by the organisation, provides opportunities for parents and whƒÅnau to contribute to the valued outcomes of the service. The organisation should draw from these national findings to inform their strategic direction.

Key Next Steps

ERO and leaders agree that their priority next steps are to:

  • encourage greater use of te reo MƒÅori

  • develop and implement a curriculum reflective of the local context

  • enhance aspects of assessment, planning and evaluation

  • strengthen the internal evaluation framework.

Management Assurance on Legal Requirements

Before the review, the staff and management of Frimley Early Learning Centre completed an ERO Centre Assurance Statement and Self-Audit Checklist. In these documents they attested that they have taken all reasonable steps to meet their legal obligations related to:

  • curriculum
  • premises and facilities
  • health and safety practices
  • governance, management and administration.

During the review, ERO looked at the service‚Äôs systems for managing the following areas that have a potentially high impact on children's wellbeing:

  • emotional safety (including positive guidance and child protection)

  • physical safety (including supervision; sleep procedures; accidents; medication; hygiene; excursion policies and procedures)

  • suitable staffing (including qualification levels; police vetting; teacher registration; ratios)

  • evacuation procedures and practices for fire and earthquake.

All early childhood services are required to promote children's health and safety and to regularly review their compliance with legal requirements.
