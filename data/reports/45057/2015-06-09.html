ERO's findings that support this overall judgement are summarised below.

Background

Bell Street Early Learning Centre is a privately owned early childhood service in Featherston. It is licensed for 23 children including up to eight up to two year olds. There are 37 children on the roll Full time care and education is offered in a mixed aged setting. Many children attend the centre part time.

The service, which opened in 2009, is led by the supervisor and assistant supervisor. Most teachers are qualified and two are currently working towards their teaching qualification.

The philosophy of the centre highlights a child-led curriculum with an emphasis on respectful and responsive relationships and the promotion of children‚Äôs wellbeing and self-management skills. It strongly reflects the principles and strands of Te WhƒÅriki, the early childhood curriculum. The premises and spacious outdoor area were purposefully chosen and developed to give the feel of a family home. A suitable environment for outdoor learning and exploration is provided.

Supervisors and teachers have responded well to the areas for development identified in the July 2012 ERO report. They have focused their professional learning and development on assessment, programme planning, self review, appraisal and strategic planning.

The Review Findings

Children participate in a child-centred, play-based programme. Teachers and children have fun together as they engage in play and learning. Children, including those up to two years, can access a range of equipment, spaces and resources as they make choices about their learning.

Positive, caring and responsive relationships are a key feature of this centre. Interactions between teachers and children are warm, nurturing and respectful. Children are confident, happy, and display a sense of belonging. Parents and families are made to feel welcome and teachers develop strong partnerships with them to support children‚Äôs learning and wellbeing.

Children are empowered to take responsibility for the care of themselves, others and the environment. The curriculum places an emphasis on the development of skills and behaviours appropriate for transition to school. Children‚Äôs social competence and self-help skills are promoted well. Teachers have identified they plan to continue to develop processes and relationships with local schools to support children‚Äôs successful transitions.

Teachers have reviewed and improved systems for the communication of programme planning. They regularly complete digital learning stories about children. These provide attractive records of children‚Äôs learning activities that can be shared with whƒÅnau. The quality and depth of many of these assessment records has improved as a result of reflection and recent professional development. Families often contribute feedback about the stories. Teachers should continue to strengthen assessment practice by ensuring records:

captures the progression and extension of children‚Äôs learning over time

celebrates children‚Äôs cultural diversity.

Te reo MƒÅori and aspects of tikanga MƒÅori are incorporated throughout the curriculum. MƒÅori values of manaakitanga and whanaungatanga are reflected strongly within the centre. A tuakana teina approach to learning is promoted. Older children have opportunities for leadership and to care for younger children.

The centre has begun to make connections with representatives of NgƒÅti Kahungunu ki Wairarapa. Managers and ERO agree that teachers should continue to develop their knowledge about local tikanga and their confidence and skill with te reo MƒÅori so they can strengthen the reflection of te ao MƒÅori throughout the curriculum.

A clear vision sets the direction for the service. Managers ensure the centre philosophy and vision are linked to a clear and useful strategic plan.

A systematic process of self review is evident. This approach is improvement focused, informed by research and considers a range of perspectives. Recent reviews have led to useful developments and improved systems. A next step is to strengthen self review so that it has a strong focus on evaluating the quality of curriculum, teaching and learning.

Managers have led a review and development of the appraisal process for teachers. The new model encourages teachers to be reflective and initiate their own professional learning. There is a sound process to support provisionally registered teachers. The inclusion of more evidence-based critical feedback about teaching is likely to improve the appraisal process further.

Key Next Steps

Managers and ERO agree the next steps for improvement are to:

  • strengthen self review and evaluation with a focus on learning outcomes for children
  • continue to develop assessment processes.

ERO and managers discussed ways in which a stronger focus on teaching and learning could be made more explicit in processes for evaluation and decision-making, such as self review, appraisal and annual planning.

Management Assurance on Legal Requirements

Before the review, the staff and management of Bell Street Early Learning Centre completed an ERO Centre Assurance Statement and Self-Audit Checklist. In these documents they attested that they have taken all reasonable steps to meet their legal obligations related to:

  • curriculum
  • premises and facilities
  • health and safety practices
  • governance, management and administration.

During the review, ERO looked at the service‚Äôs systems for managing the following areas that have a potentially high impact on children's wellbeing:

  • emotional safety (including positive guidance and child protection)
  • physical safety (including supervision; sleep procedures; accidents; medication; hygiene; excursion policies and procedures)
  • suitable staffing (including qualification levels; police vetting; teacher registration; ratios)
  • evacuation procedures and practices for fire and earthquake.

All early childhood services are required to promote children's health and safety and to regularly review their compliance with legal requirements.
