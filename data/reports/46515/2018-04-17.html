Background

Nurtured at Home is a privately owned company providing home-based care for children in the wider Auckland, Waikato, Bay of Plenty, Hawkes Bay and Gisborne areas. The Bay of plenty service includes two networks across the region. Nurtured at Home (Bay) 1 network is a standard-funded network that includes both trained and untrained educators. Each educator cares for up to four pre-school aged children at any one time in the educator's home. The network is licensed for 80 children including 40 up to the age of two years. Currently there are 43 enrolled including 17 MƒÅori children.

The owner is a qualified and experienced early childhood teacher. She provides governance and leadership support for the network. The leadership structure includes a service manager and a team providing administrative, financial, training and development and quality assurance support to the networks.

Nurtured at Home (Bay) 1 currently employs one fully-registered teacher. The visiting teacher takes responsibility for up to 18 educators and the children in their care.

The service philosophy and vision aims to provide high-quality early childhood learning in safe, nurturing and fun home environments. The service recognises five guiding principles that include fostering a love of learning, partnerships that enable children to reach their full potential, nurturing, safe places to work, being a leader of best practice and recognising and valuing the importance of the youngest children in society.

This is the first review for this network.

The Review Findings

Educators are providing a safe, positive and nurturing environment for children. Their homes provide interesting and varied learning environments for children. Many educators are responsive to the languages and cultures of children and families. The service takes a considered and culturally responsive approach to the placement of children with educators, especially where additional needs are identified on enrolment. Visiting teachers use their observations of children in the educator's home to give guidance and advice, and recommend any necessary referrals when special educational or developmental needs are identified.

Children observed by ERO at the playgroup were confident and settled. Visiting teachers and educators know children well and warmly welcome them to the playgroup sessions. Children of mixed ages are encouraged to make choices and play independently or in small groups. Children also participate in regular outings and trips organised by the service. Younger children have access to an appropriate range of materials and equipment that support their literacy and mathematics learning.

Children up to the age of two years, experience respectful, nurturing interactions during care moments such as feeding and nappy changes. Records show that care routines are responsive to the individual needs and rhythms of children. Babies benefit from the calm and unhurried practice of familiar and trusted educators. These interactions effectively promote their wellbeing and belonging. Older children have frequent opportunities to build leadership and take responsibility as they interact with younger children in family-like settings. These experiences support the development of their social competencies and self-esteem.

Educators are well supported to build their professional skills and capabilities. As part of their training programme, educators have opportunities to participate in workshops to build their knowledge and understanding of good practice and care for children. Educators recently participated in workshops to build their understanding of the revised Te WhƒÅriki, the New Zealand curriculum for early childhood.

Visiting teachers maintain comprehensive records of their visits and identify emerging interests and strengths of individual children. They provide a variety of resources and materials to support children's learning. Educators and visiting teachers document children's learning and progress in individual portfolios, which are valued by families and increasingly accessible to them online.

MƒÅori children and their families experience respectful and responsive care in educator homes. Visiting teachers provide information and workshops to build educator understanding of MƒÅori values and perspectives. The service recognises the need to continue to develop and implement a local, bicultural curriculum and embed practices that reflect the cultural values of MƒÅori whƒÅnau and families and promote success for MƒÅori children.

Visiting teachers provide strong leadership as they respond to the differing needs of each educator in their home setting. They provide regular feedback to educators about their practice, offer advice and guidance where it is needed and monitor health and safety practices in educator homes. In addition, educators can attend regular playgroups and participate in planned trips and outings.

A next step for the service is to strengthen the intentional planning, assessment and documentation of children's learning. Visiting teachers and educators need to ensure planning adds complexity and interest to meet the needs of all children's learning. They need to enhance the learning partnership with families in relation to their child's programme.

Leadership models reflective practice, and places a high priority on establishing meaningful, respectful relationships throughout the network. They take additional responsibilities for overseeing self-review practices for the organisation nationally. Leadership is collaborative and collegial. They have established a useful annual in-house conference that provides professional learning for visiting teachers.

The service benefits from the governance support provided by the owner and leaders. Leadership and management roles and responsibilities are clearly defined and adapted to meet the needs of the service. Comprehensive policies, procedures guide the services day-to-day operations and enable them to meet regulatory requirements. A particular strength is the commitment of the leaders to reduce barriers to participation and promote equitable opportunities for all families to access home-based care.

Key Next Steps

The owner and leaders should continue to build the effectiveness of self review by developing shared and agreed criteria of best practice to guide the ongoing development of the service and better enable it to meet the intent of the Mission statement and guiding principles.

Consideration should be given to:

  • a review of the service's overarching vision and philosophy to better reflect the bicultural heritage of Aotearoa New Zealand

  • enriching the learning partnership amongst educators, families and visiting teachers.
