Background

Bright Horizons Drury is located in a semi-rural setting near Auckland‚Äôs southern motorway in Drury. The centre is licensed to provide full day and school length day education and care sessions for 46 children, including 24 up to the age of two years. The centre has a small number of MƒÅori families. Children from Dutch, British and Chinese families also attend. Children with specific learning abilities are well supported in the programme. The centre‚Äôs philosophy has a focus on collaborative relationships encouraging children‚Äôs exploration, independence, and confidence, and programmes planned to extending children‚Äôs individual and group interests.

The centre is one of two centres that are privately owned by an Australian early childhood organisation, Bright Horizons New Zealand Childcare Limited. A regional manager oversees the centre and works closely with the centre director, who is supported in the day-to-day running of the centre by the head teacher.

The 2012 ERO report suggested that the following areas could continue to be strengthened by:

  • recording how teachers intend to build on children‚Äôs individual interests, capabilities and cultural heritages
  • increasing the visibility of planning documents for children and parents
  • enriching the learning environments by providing a greater selection of resources and experiences to challenge children‚Äôs learning.

The 2012 ERO report noted that the centre managers intended to continue to strengthen self-review practices. ERO also recommended that long term plans be developed to guide the future development of the centre and that action plans be developed to address priorities. Managers and teachers have responded positively to ERO‚Äôs recommendations and have implemented improvements.

The Review Findings

Children are friendly, articulate and are developing sound social skills. They manage their own care routines capably and are developing self-help skills. Consideration and empathy for others are evident in the centre. Respect and trust underpin relationships.

Younger children are supported by teachers to develop at their own pace and to build their language proficiency. Older children are developing good communication skills and readily express their opinions and ideas to each other and with teachers. All children display a strong sense of belonging and wellbeing.

Teachers know the children and their families well. They are aware of individual children‚Äôs strengths and abilities. Teachers provide opportunities for children to revisit their learning experiences. Programme plans are clearly displayed and show how teachers are providing activities and experiences to extend learning. Many parents share their specialist knowledge and expertise with children in the programme.

The learning environments have been improved and children have access to a wide range of resources. Teachers are able to adapt the programme and learning environments to support children‚Äôs new interests.

Teacher appraisal systems have been improved. The system enables managers and teachers to reflect on agreed goals more frequently. It also has the potential to strengthen teachers‚Äô leadership skills, professional knowledge and practice.

Key Next Steps

Centre managers agree that it would be useful to:

  • continue to develop tikanga and te reo MƒÅori in the programme
  • continue to encourage staff to use appraisal processes to reflect critically on the effectiveness of their teaching and learning practices
  • continue to develop research practices and high quality indicators of good practice that add depth to learning and bring about high levels of improvement in self review.

Management Assurance on Legal Requirements

Before the review, the staff and management of Bright Horizons Drury completed an ERO Centre Assurance Statement and Self-Audit Checklist. In these documents they attested that they have taken all reasonable steps to meet their legal obligations related to:

  • curriculum
  • premises and facilities
  • health and safety practices
  • governance, management and administration.

During the review, ERO looked at the service‚Äôs systems for managing the following areas that have a potentially high impact on children's wellbeing:

  • emotional safety (including positive guidance and child protection)
  • physical safety (including supervision; sleep procedures; accidents; medication; hygiene; excursion policies and procedures)
  • suitable staffing (including qualification levels; police vetting; teacher registration; ratios)
  • evacuation procedures and practices for fire and earthquake.

All early childhood services are required to promote children's health and safety and to regularly review their compliance with legal requirements.
