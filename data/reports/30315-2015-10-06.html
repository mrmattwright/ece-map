1 Evaluation of Bright Stars Educare Limited

ERO's findings that support this overall judgement are summarised below.

Background

Bright Stars Educare is an all-day early childhood education and care centre located in the Hamilton suburb of Deanwell. It is licensed for 25 children, with a maximum of five children up to two years of age. At the time of this ERO review, 36 children were enrolled, of whom 22 were identified as MƒÅori, and 13 as New Zealand European/PƒÅkehƒÅ.

The centre views whakawhanaungatanga as a basic value of the service. It aims to build meaningful relationships with the wider community, hap≈´ and iwi. There is an expectation that children will learn life skills and values, and gain confidence through child and teacher-initiated activities linked to Te WhƒÅriki, the early childhood curriculum. MƒÅori language is used naturally in the programme and at mat times, where approximately half the spoken language is MƒÅori.

The centre is privately owned, and is one of two services managed by the owner. It employs both three qualified teachers, two of whom are MƒÅori, and one of Pacific heritage. The centre is led by an experienced head teacher, and functions predominately as a mixed age-group.

The centre has a positive reporting history with ERO. Since the last ERO review, the outdoor area has been substantially improved to provide an attractive space that offers rich play opportunities. Leaders and teachers have also concentrated on increasing parent involvement by providing whƒÅnau gatherings and encouraging parents and families to spend time in the centre.

The Review Findings

Children experience warm, positive relationships and interactions with teachers, and with other children. Tuakana-teina relationships are strong, and older children are encouraged to assist toddlers with self-care activities and to include them in their play. Children are growing as confident learners, who show initiative and engagement in learning.

The environment is well prepared to offer a range of interesting activities and children also enjoy exploring the world of nature. Sustained active play using balls and tricycles is highly evident, as is imaginative play using cars, construction equipment and dress-ups. Children have fun.

Teachers support children‚Äôs communication skills through many sustained conversations and interactions that encourage them to share and extend their ideas. MƒÅtauranga MƒÅori is integrated in the programme, particularly during well-managed mat times, which include waiata, songs, stories, and the extensive use of te reo and tikanga MƒÅori. MƒÅori children and their families display a strong sense of belonging. Children‚Äôs home languages are valued and affirmed.

The centre has undertaken a useful review of the environment that has prompted teachers to provide dedicated space for the needs of infants and young toddlers. Four-year-olds can also choose to spend uninterrupted time interacting and exploring activities that include a literacy focus. Books are read and shared, and children enjoy drawing and creating.

Teachers have developed a useful approach to support children's transition to school. Children are developing social skills and a sense of empathy and confidence while exploring early literacy and numeracy concepts.

Teachers write learning stories that notice, recognise and respond to children‚Äôs learning. Children enjoy sharing stories and photos in their portfolios, and these provide a record of their involvement in the programme. Portfolios are also used to report to parents. Teachers know children well, and share their professional knowledge and understandings during staff meetings.

The programme builds on children‚Äôs interests and ability to manage their own learning and takes account of parents‚Äô aspirations. Teachers recognise children with diverse learning needs, especially in language, and work hard to strengthen their skills and understandings. They prepare the environment to allow children to follow their interests, use the internet to research information, and develop teacher-initiated events and activities. Trips in the local area use buses, and focuses on events such as Matariki and the Balloon Festival. These experiences enrich and extend children‚Äôs learning.

Centre leaders, together with the manager, have created and retained a team of teachers who bring varied skills, strengths, interests and personalities to the centre. Teachers readily accept opportunities to take up leadership responsibilities.

The centre has a low turnover of staff, and the team is committed to the philosophy and vision of the service, which they see as a home away from home. Teachers warmly welcome parents, and value their participation, opinions and aspirations for their children.

The service manager has undertaken professional development and has developed a strategic plan to provide direction for future development. With further refinement to the goals, the plan should provide more measurable objectives for implementing relevant actions, and evaluate progress against these objectives. Attention should also be given to strengthening teacher appraisal to take account of practising teacher criteria.

Key Next Steps

ERO identified, and leaders and teachers agree, that the next step for the centre is to strengthen planning and assessment. Consideration needs to be given to:

  • including information from parent aspirations and next steps in learning stories to respond to children‚Äôs individual learning
  • making links between learning stories to show children‚Äôs ongoing development and progress.

The owner should continue to strengthen delegation practices and communication with leaders to promote smoother processes of centre management and organisation.

Management Assurance on Legal Requirements

Before the review, the staff and management of Bright Stars Educare Limited completed an ERO Centre Assurance Statement and Self-Audit Checklist. In these documents they attested that they have taken all reasonable steps to meet their legal obligations related to:

  • curriculum
  • premises and facilities
  • health and safety practices
  • governance, management and administration.

During the review, ERO looked at the service‚Äôs systems for managing the following areas that have a potentially high impact on children's wellbeing:

  • emotional safety (including positive guidance and child protection)
  • physical safety (including supervision; sleep procedures; accidents; medication; hygiene; excursion policies and procedures)
  • suitable staffing (including qualification levels; police vetting; teacher registration; ratios)
  • evacuation procedures and practices for fire and earthquake.

All early childhood services are required to promote children's health and safety and to regularly review their compliance with legal requirements.

To improve current practice, the service provider must ensure that parents/caregivers have given prior written approval to their child‚Äôs participation and of the proposed ratio for special excursions, prior to the excursion taking place.
